# 02 – Decision Framework

## Purpose

This document defines the decision-making framework for every wardrobe recommendation and purchase.

Its purpose is to ensure that recommendations remain consistent regardless of brand, fashion trends, budget, or individual garments.

This framework should be followed before evaluating any clothing, footwear, accessory, or wardrobe purchase.

---

# Core Principle

The objective is **not to buy the best garment.**

The objective is to make the best decision for the wardrobe as a whole.

Every purchase should strengthen the system rather than simply add another item.

---

# Decision Hierarchy

When evaluating any garment, criteria should always be considered in the following order.

## 1. Silhouette

Highest priority.

The garment should improve or maintain the intended visual shape.

Silhouette always takes precedence over colour, fabric, price or brand.

Questions:

* Does this strengthen the intended masculine silhouette?
* Does it improve body proportions?
* Does it create unnecessary visual imbalance?

---

## 2. Proportions

The garment should support balanced visual proportions.

Examples:

* Appropriate garment length
* Correct rise
* Balanced leg opening
* Appropriate collar size

A well-proportioned garment is preferred over a trend-driven silhouette.

---

## 3. Fit

Once silhouette is correct, evaluate sizing.

The garment should fit comfortably without excess tension or unnecessary volume.

Fit should never compensate for poor silhouette.

---

## 4. Wardrobe Role

Every garment must perform one primary role.

Examples:

* Office shirt
* Casual trouser
* Summer layer
* Date-night top
* Everyday sneaker

Avoid purchasing garments that duplicate existing roles unless replacing an inferior item.

---

## 5. Versatility

Evaluate how easily the garment integrates into the existing wardrobe.

Questions:

* How many outfits does it create?
* How many existing garments does it complement?
* Can it be worn across multiple occasions?

Versatility is measured by real outfit creation rather than theoretical combinations.

---

## 6. Colour Harmony

Evaluate colour only after silhouette, fit and role have been satisfied.

Priority:

* Warm
* Muted
* Earthy
* Deep Autumn compatible

Colour should support the wardrobe rather than dominate it.

---

## 7. Fabric & Texture

Prefer fabrics that provide:

* Structure
* Durability
* Comfort
* Appropriate seasonal performance
* Visual richness

Texture should create interest before pattern.

---

## 8. Construction Quality

Evaluate:

* Stitching
* Collar construction
* Fabric stability
* Buttons
* Hardware
* Durability
* Finishing

Construction quality determines longevity.

---

## 9. Price-to-Value

Evaluate value rather than price.

Higher prices are acceptable only when accompanied by meaningful improvements in quality, longevity or versatility.

---

## 10. Brand

Lowest priority.

Brands should never compensate for weaknesses in any higher-level criterion.

---

# Purchase Algorithm

Before purchasing any item, answer the following questions in order.

## Step 1

Is this objectively a well-designed garment?

If no:

Reject.

---

## Step 2

Does it suit my body type?

If no:

Reject.

---

## Step 3

Does it suit my colour palette?

If no:

Reject unless a compelling reason exists.

---

## Step 4

Does it fill an existing wardrobe gap?

If no:

Proceed only if replacing an inferior item.

---

## Step 5

Does it significantly increase outfit possibilities?

If no:

Reject.

---

## Step 6

Is this the highest-return purchase available within the current budget?

If no:

Delay purchase.

---

## Step 7

Would I still want this if there were no discounts or brand recognition?

If no:

Reject.

---

# Opportunity Cost Principle

Every purchase consumes finite budget, wardrobe space and attention.

Always compare a potential purchase against the highest-impact alternative.

Example:

Do not compare:

"This shirt vs no shirt."

Compare:

"This shirt vs olive chinos."

"This shirt vs dark denim."

"This shirt vs saving for better footwear."

---

# Wardrobe Role Principle

Every garment should have one clearly defined primary role.

Possible roles include:

* Professional
* Casual
* Smart Casual
* Evening
* Travel
* Layering
* Summer
* Winter
* Gym

Avoid multiple garments serving identical roles unless replacing an older item.

---

# Rejection Rules

Reject immediately if any of the following apply:

* Poor silhouette
* Poor shoulder fit
* Skinny fit
* Extreme oversized fit
* Loud branding
* Excessive graphics
* Distressing
* Artificial fading
* Trend-driven design
* Poor colour harmony
* Requires multiple additional purchases before becoming wearable

---

# Replacement Rules

Replace an existing garment instead of expanding the wardrobe when:

* The existing garment no longer fits.
* Construction has deteriorated.
* Colour no longer aligns with the wardrobe.
* The new garment performs the same role significantly better.
* The replacement meaningfully increases versatility.

---

# Trade-off Rules

When conflicts occur, follow this order:

Silhouette > Fit > Wardrobe Role > Versatility > Colour > Fabric > Construction > Price > Brand

Examples:

* Better silhouette outweighs a slightly less ideal colour.
* Better versatility outweighs trendiness.
* Better construction outweighs brand prestige.
* Better role fulfilment outweighs having another colour option.

---

# Decision Outcomes

Every recommendation should end with one of the following.

## Buy

Strongly satisfies the framework.

High priority.

Excellent use of budget.

---

## Consider

Good option.

Trade-offs exist.

Worth evaluating against alternatives.

---

## Skip

Fails one or more high-priority criteria.

Opportunity cost is too high.

---

# Long-Term Philosophy

The wardrobe should become easier to wear with every purchase.

Each new garment should:

* Increase cohesion.
* Increase outfit quality.
* Reduce decision fatigue.
* Strengthen the intended personal image.

The objective is not to own more clothing.

The objective is to own a wardrobe that consistently performs its intended function with minimal compromise.

## Permanent Footwear Constraints

### Foot Profile

- Wide forefoot (metatarsal area)
- Above-average toe splay
- Medium-to-high instep
- Big toe and second toe approximately equal length

### Common Fit Issues

- Toe box feels cramped before shoe length becomes insufficient.
- Pressure develops across the top of the foot.
- Standard-width shoes often require sizing up unnecessarily.

### Decision Rules

Priority order:

1. Last shape
2. Forefoot width
3. Instep volume
4. Overall fit
5. Size

Never size up before confirming that the last is appropriate.

A correctly shaped last in the proper size is preferred over a longer shoe with an unsuitable last.

### Avoid

- Narrow Italian lasts
- Aggressive almond or pointed toes
- Low-volume shoes
- Narrow toe boxes
- Shoes known to run narrow

### Prefer

- Anatomically generous toe boxes
- Medium or wide lasts
- Moderate toe taper
- Medium-to-high instep volume

## Footwear Decision Framework

### Evaluation Priority

When evaluating footwear, use the following order:

1. Foot compatibility
2. Last & Shape
3. Construction
4. Upper Material
5. Comfort
6. Sole
7. Colour
8. Brand

### Foot Compatibility Rules

For my foot shape:

* Prioritize forefoot width before overall length.
* Prioritize adequate instep volume before aesthetics.
* Never size up before determining whether the last is appropriate.
* Reject shoes with narrow or aggressively tapered toe boxes.
* Avoid lasts known to run narrow.
* Prefer medium or wide-fitting lasts with generous forefoot volume.

### Recommendation Rules

Every footwear recommendation should include:

* Width compatibility
* Last characteristics
* Whether the model runs narrow, standard or wide
* Sizing advice (only after assessing width)
* Confidence that the shoe suits my foot profile
