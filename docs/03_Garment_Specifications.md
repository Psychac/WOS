# 03 – Garment Specifications

## Purpose

This document defines the characteristics of well-designed garments and explains how to evaluate them.

It is a permanent reference manual and does not contain shopping priorities or brand recommendations.

---

# Part I — Garment Fundamentals

This section defines concepts that apply across multiple garment categories.

## 1. Formality Scale

Every garment exists somewhere on a spectrum rather than in a fixed category.

- Formal
- Business
- Business Casual
- Smart Casual
- Casual
- Relaxed Casual

A garment should be evaluated within its intended level of formality rather than against garments serving different purposes.

---

## 2. Fit

Fit describes how a garment follows the body's dimensions.

A A good fit should:

- Allow unrestricted movement
- Follow the body's shape without excessive tightness
- Avoid unnecessary excess fabric
- Support the intended silhouette

Fit is independent of style.

A relaxed-fit garment can fit correctly.

A slim-fit garment can fit poorly.

---

## 3. Silhouette

Silhouette is the visual outline created by the body and clothing together.

Silhouette is more important than numerical size.

The desired silhouette should support the wearer's proportions and intended style identity.

---

## 4. Structure

Structure is the ability of a garment to create or maintain shape.

Sources of structure include:

- Fabric weight
- Interfacing
- Construction
- Collar design
- Shoulder design

Greater structure generally increases visual authority and masculinity, while excessive structure can reduce comfort and appear overly formal.

---

## 5. Drape

Drape describes how fabric hangs on the body.

Good drape should follow the intended silhouette without clinging or collapsing.

---

## 6. Visual Weight

Visual weight is the perceived prominence of a garment.

It is influenced by:

- Colour value
- Texture
- Fabric weight
- Pattern
- Volume

Visual weight should be distributed intentionally to create balanced proportions.

---

## 7. Texture

Texture creates visual interest without relying on loud patterns.

Examples include:

- Oxford cloth
- Twill
- Linen
- Corduroy
- Brushed cotton

Texture is generally preferred over bold prints for long-term versatility.

---

## 8. Construction Quality

Construction determines durability, shape retention and finishing quality.

Indicators include:

- Stitching consistency
- Pattern alignment
- Reinforcement
- Hardware quality
- Seam finishing

Construction should be evaluated independently from branding.

---

## 9. Wardrobe Role

Every garment should perform one primary function within the wardrobe.

Examples:

- Office foundation
- Everyday casual
- Evening smart casual
- Travel
- Seasonal layering

Garments without a clear role should generally be avoided.

---

## 10. Versatility

Versatility is the ability of a garment to integrate naturally into multiple outfits and occasions.

It should be evaluated by practical use rather than theoretical combinations.


# Part II – Shirts

## Philosophy

Shirts define the upper body of an outfit. They frame the face, influence perceived shoulder width, communicate formality, and provide the primary visual connection between wearer and observer.

For this wardrobe, shirts should prioritize silhouette, versatility, and timeless design over trend-driven styling.

---

## Universal Principles

* Prioritize fit before colour.
* Prioritize collar before brand.
* Prioritize fabric before pattern.
* Texture is preferable to loud patterns.
* A shirt should support the outfit rather than dominate it.
* Every shirt should have a clearly defined wardrobe role.
* Choose timeless designs over trend-driven details.

---

## Foundation vs Accent

### Foundation Shirts

Foundation shirts form the core of the wardrobe and should maximise versatility.

Characteristics:

* Timeless colours
* Minimal patterns
* High outfit compatibility
* Suitable for frequent wear

Examples:

* Formal Shirts
* Oxford Cloth Button-Downs
* Casual Button-Downs

### Accent Shirts

Accent shirts introduce seasonal variety, texture or casual character.

Characteristics:

* Seasonal fabrics
* Greater visual texture
* Lower wear frequency
* More specialised use

Examples:

* Linen Shirts
* Flannel Shirts
* Chambray Shirts
* Denim Shirts

---

## Common Pitfalls

These issues appear across multiple shirt types and should be avoided:

* **Loud branding** — Undermines timelessness and visual refinement
* **Polyester or synthetic-rich blends** — Reduces durability, breathability, and visual quality
* **Excessive decorative detailing** — Conflicts with the principle that a shirt should support the outfit, not dominate it
* **Poor shoulder fit** — Creates an unflattering silhouette regardless of other attributes

When evaluating any shirt, check these universal criteria first before considering garment-specific attributes.

# Part III — Trousers

## Trouser Philosophy

Trousers are the visual foundation of an outfit. They determine overall proportions, influence perceived height, and contribute significantly to silhouette.

Unlike shirts, poor trouser fit is difficult to disguise.

## Universal Principles

- Prioritize rise before colour.
- Prioritize fit before fabric.
- Prioritize silhouette before trend.
- Never compromise on rise or fit for a more desirable colour.
- Choose timeless cuts over trend-driven silhouettes.
- Trousers should create balance rather than draw attention.
- Buy jeans for how they'll age, not how they look on day one.

These principles apply to every trouser category in this document.

## Foundation vs Accent

### Foundation Trousers

Foundation trousers should maximize versatility and outfit compatibility.

Characteristics:

Accent trousers introduce texture, seasonality or visual interest.

Characteristics:

Seasonal materials
Greater visual texture
Lower wardrobe frequency

Examples:

Corduroy Trousers

## Common Pitfalls

These issues appear across multiple trouser types:

* **Low or very low rise** — Affects proportions and requires constant adjustment
* **Skinny or overly tapered fit** — Conflicts with the goal of a clean, balanced silhouette
* **Polyester-dominant fabrics** — Reduces durability and breathability; lacks natural drape
* **Decorative details or loud branding** — Undermines the principle that trousers should provide a neutral foundation
* **Trend-driven cuts** — Compromises long-term versatility

# Part IV – Knit Tops

## Philosophy

Knit tops provide texture, softness and visual depth while occupying the space between shirts and outerwear.

For this wardrobe, knit tops should prioritize refined simplicity, tactile fabrics and versatility over loud branding or trend-driven styling.

---

## Universal Principles

- Prioritize texture over graphics.
- Prioritize natural fibres over synthetics.
- Fit should skim the body rather than cling.
- Heavy logos and graphics reduce versatility.
- Knitwear should complement, not compete with, the rest of the outfit.

---

## Foundation vs Accent

### Foundation Knit Tops

Designed for frequent wear and maximum versatility.

Examples:

- Polo Shirts
- Knit Polos
- Crewneck Sweaters

### Accent Knit Tops

Used to introduce seasonal texture or layering.

Examples:

- Quarter-Zips
- Cardigans

## Common Pitfalls

These issues appear across multiple knit types:

* **Heavy logos and bold graphics** — Reduces versatility and violates the principle that knitwear should complement, not compete
* **Loud synthetic finishes** — Conflicts with the priority of natural fibres and tactile quality
* **Bold or high-contrast stripes** — Creates visual competition with the outfit
* **Clingy or oversized fits** — Should skim the body, not emphasize shape or create shapelessness

# Part V – Outerwear

## Philosophy

Outerwear is the outermost visual layer of an outfit and has a disproportionate influence on first impressions.

Unlike shirts and trousers, outerwear should be evaluated not only on its own merits, but also on how it enhances the garments beneath it.

For this wardrobe, outerwear should prioritize silhouette, structure and timeless design over trend-driven styling.

---

## Universal Principles

* Prioritize silhouette before colour.
* Prioritize layering capability before visual details.
* Prioritize structure before decoration.
* Texture is preferable to bold patterns.
* Outerwear should enhance the outfit rather than dominate it.
* Every jacket should have a clearly defined wardrobe role.
* Buy fewer, better jackets rather than many similar ones.

---

## Foundation vs Accent

### Foundation Outerwear

Versatile jackets that integrate naturally with the majority of the wardrobe.

Characteristics:

* Timeless silhouettes
* Minimal detailing
* High outfit compatibility
* Suitable for frequent wear

Examples:

* Overshirt
* Harrington Jacket
* Suede Jacket

### Accent Outerwear

Seasonal or specialised pieces that add texture, utility or visual character.

Characteristics:

* Lower wear frequency
* More specific styling purpose
* Greater seasonal dependence

Examples:

* Chore Jacket
* Field Jacket
* Bomber Jacket
* Overcoat

---

## Common Pitfalls

These issues appear across multiple outerwear types:

* **Bold patterns or graphic prints** — Reduces versatility and visual restraint; conflicts with prioritizing texture
* **Heavy branding or decorative detailing** — Prioritize structure before decoration; detracts from clean silhouette
* **Trend-driven designs** — Timeless silhouettes are essential for a wardrobe investment
* **Poor shoulder or chest fit** — Outerwear must enhance the silhouette beneath it, not fight it
* **Oversized or undersized proportions** — Should coordinate naturally with layered clothing underneath

# Part VI – Tailoring

## Philosophy

Tailoring is the most structured category in the wardrobe and represents the highest level of refinement.

Unlike casual clothing, tailoring should enhance the natural proportions of the body through precise fit, clean lines, and thoughtful construction.

For this wardrobe, tailoring should prioritize proportion, structure, and timeless elegance over fashion trends.

---

## Universal Principles

* Prioritize shoulder fit above all else.
* Prioritize silhouette before colour.
* Structure should enhance—not exaggerate—the physique.
* Natural fabrics are strongly preferred.
* Buy fewer, higher-quality tailoring pieces.
* Classic proportions age better than fashion-forward cuts.

---

## Foundation vs Accent

### Foundation Tailoring

The core tailoring pieces suitable for most formal and business occasions.

Examples:

* Navy Suit
* Blazer

### Accent Tailoring

Specialized garments for specific occasions or personal expression.

Examples:

* Sport Coat
* Waistcoat
* Tuxedo

---

## Common Pitfalls

These issues appear across multiple tailoring types:

* **Poor shoulder fit** — The foundation of all tailoring; cannot be corrected elsewhere
* **Fashion-forward or oversized cuts** — Classic proportions outlast trends and suit various body types better
* **Heavy branding or decorative buttons** — Detracts from clean lines and timeless elegance
* **Synthetic or low-quality fabrics** — Natural fabrics drape better and age with character
* **Poor construction** — Half-canvas or better is essential for longevity and shape retention

# Part VII – Footwear

## Philosophy

Footwear is the foundation of every outfit and has a disproportionate influence on perceived formality, refinement and visual balance.

Unlike clothing, shoes should be evaluated primarily by their shape, construction and materials rather than branding or trend.

For this wardrobe, footwear should prioritize timeless design, versatility and longevity.

---

## Universal Principles

* Prioritize shape before colour.
* Prioritize construction before brand.
* Prioritize leather quality before finish.
* Buy fewer, higher-quality pairs.
* Avoid trend-driven silhouettes.
* Shoes should complement the outfit rather than dominate it.

---

## Foundation vs Accent

### Foundation Footwear

Shoes worn frequently across multiple occasions.

Characteristics:

* Maximum versatility
* Timeless styling
* Neutral colours
* High outfit compatibility

Examples:

* Minimal Leather Sneakers
* Penny Loafers
* Derby Shoes

### Accent Footwear

Shoes intended for specific seasons or occasions.

Characteristics:

* Lower wear frequency
* More specialized styling

Examples:

* Chelsea Boots
* Chukka Boots
* Oxford Shoes
* Sandals

---

## Common Pitfalls

These issues appear across multiple footwear types:

* **Trend-driven silhouettes** — Dated shoe shapes look outdated quickly; timeless lasts endure
* **Poor construction** — Weak stitching, thin soles, or inferior welting compromises longevity
* **Synthetic or faux leather** — Doesn't age well or develop patina; cracks and peels
* **Excessive detailing or branding** — Should complement the outfit, not draw attention
* **Incompatible toe shapes or proportions** — Extreme pointed or oversized toes conflict with modern minimalist aesthetics

# Part VIII – Accessories

## Philosophy

Accessories complete an outfit through subtle refinement rather than visual prominence.

For this wardrobe, accessories should reinforce cohesion, functionality and timelessness while avoiding unnecessary attention.

---

## Universal Principles

* Accessories should complement rather than dominate.
* Prioritize quality over quantity.
* Leather should coordinate across the outfit where practical.
* Metal finishes should remain consistent within an outfit.
* Avoid trend-driven statement accessories.
* Every accessory should have a functional purpose.

---

## Foundation vs Accent

### Foundation Accessories

Frequently used essentials that support nearly every outfit.

Examples:

* Leather Belt
* Watch
* Sunglasses

### Accent Accessories

Occasional pieces that introduce seasonal function or subtle personality.

Examples:

* Scarf
* Pocket Square
* Gloves
* Cap

---

## Common Pitfalls

These issues appear across multiple accessory types:

* **Heavy branding or logos** — Detracts from the principle that accessories should complement, not dominate
* **Trend-driven designs** — Statement accessories date quickly; timeless pieces remain relevant
* **Synthetic or low-quality materials** — Reduces longevity and professional appearance
* **Incompatible metal finishes** — Creates visual discord when mixed with existing accessories
* **Excessive detailing** — Conflicts with the goal of subtle refinement

---
